Country Tips

Republic of South Africa
  • Landscape: Extremely diverse. The west features arid desert and semi-desert (e.g., the Karoo and Northern Cape), while the east has lush subtropical regions (KwaZulu-Natal, parts of Mpumalanga). The Drakensberg mountains run along the east, offering dramatic elevation changes. The interior (Highveld) is a plateau with grasslands and major cities. Coastal regions vary from Mediterranean (Cape Town) to humid subtropical.
  • Street View Coverage: Extensive. South Africa was the first African country with full Street View coverage, launched in 2010. Major highways, cities, towns, and tourist destinations are well-covered. Even rural roads, national parks, and scenic drives (e.g., Garden Route, Chapman's Peak) are often viewable.
  • Road Signage:
    • National Roads: Labeled "N" + number (e.g., N1, N2, N3). Green signs with white text. These are major highways connecting provinces and cities.
    • Provincial Roads: Labeled "R" + number (e.g., R21, R44). Blue signs with white text. These roads connect towns and regions within provinces.
    • Metropolitan/Minor Roads: May also use "M" + number format (e.g., M1 in Johannesburg), especially in major metro areas. These signs are usually green or blue.
    • City Streets: In cities like Cape Town, Johannesburg, or Durban, streets are well-labeled with names in English, Afrikaans, Zulu, or Xhosa (e.g., "Voortrekker Rd", "Umhlanga Rocks Dr"). Signage is generally good.
  • Driving Side: Left-hand traffic.
Botswana
  • Landscape: Mostly flat and semi-arid, with large expanses of savannah and bushland. The Kalahari Desert covers much of the west and south. The north features more greenery, including the Okavango Delta and Chobe National Park, with wetlands and wildlife. Urban areas are scattered and surrounded by open terrain.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Google Street View coverage launched around 2017. Good coverage on major roads between cities like Gaborone, Francistown, Maun, and Kasane. Coverage also includes many national parks and tourist routes (especially near Okavango Delta and Chobe). Rural roads and remote villages often have limited or no coverage.
  • Road Signage:
    • Main Roads: Marked with "A" + number (e.g., A1, A3). These are tarred highways connecting major towns and border crossings.
    • Secondary Roads: Marked with "B" + number (e.g., B6, B300), typically narrower and may be paved or gravel.
    • Local Roads: Often unsigned, especially in rural or remote areas. Signage is more consistent near cities.
    • City Streets: In Gaborone and larger towns, streets are named in English, commonly with suffixes like "Rd." or "Ave" (e.g., "Nelson Mandela Rd", "Airport Rd"). Street signs are present but not always well maintained.
  • Driving Side: Left-hand traffic.
Republic of Kenya
  • Landscape: Highly varied. The central highlands around Nairobi are elevated and green, with tea and coffee plantations. The Great Rift Valley cuts through the west with dramatic escarpments and lakes. The coast (e.g., Mombasa) is low-lying, humid, and tropical. The north and northeast are arid and semi-desert. Mount Kenya (5,199 m) is the second-highest peak in Africa and dominates the central landscape.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Google Street View coverage began around 2015. Major cities like Nairobi, Mombasa, Kisumu, Nakuru, and Eldoret have extensive coverage. Major highways such as A104 (Nairobi–Nakuru–Eldoret) and A2 (towards Ethiopia) are also covered. Rural areas, parks, and remote regions have limited or no coverage. Some national parks like Nairobi National Park are viewable from adjacent roads.
  • Road Signage:
    • National Roads: Labeled "A" + number (e.g., A1, A2, A104). These are the main highways, often with green signs and white text. Well-maintained near major cities, deteriorates in rural areas.
    • Secondary Roads: Labeled "B" + number (e.g., B6, B50), connecting smaller towns. Signage is inconsistent, especially in rural areas.
    • City Streets: In Nairobi and Mombasa, streets are labeled in English, using names like "Moi Avenue", "Haile Selassie Avenue", or "Ngong Rd". Street signage exists but may be damaged or missing in older districts.
  • Specific Identifiers:
    • Vehicle with a "snorkel" or mirror
    • Yellow central road markings
    • Metal roofs of buildings
  • Driving Side: Left-hand traffic.
Republic of Ghana
  • Landscape: Tropical, green areas in the south, drier areas in the north.
  • Street View Coverage: Limited, mainly major roads and cities.
  • Specific Identifiers:
    • Vehicle with black tape on right front side of roof rack
    • White road markings
    • Red clay soil in the central and eastern parts
Republic of Senegal
  • Landscape: Variable, with characteristic "rifts".
  • Street View Coverage: Along main roads and in cities.
  • Specific Identifiers:
    • Vehicle with characteristic "rifts"
    • White road markings
    • Taxis with black and yellow colors
Federal Republic of Nigeria
  • Landscape: Flat in the north, tropical in the south.
  • Street View Coverage: Major cities and significant roads, excluding northern areas.
  • Specific Identifiers:
    • Vehicle with protective escort
    • White road markings
    • Colorful street signs in different cities
Republic of Tunisia
  • Landscape: Diverse – the north is hilly and green, especially around the Tell Atlas mountains. The central region is more arid and flat with farmland. The south transitions into desert (Sahara), with rocky plateaus and oases. Coastal regions like Sfax, Sousse, and Hammamet have Mediterranean landscapes.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Street View launched in 2014. Good coverage in major cities like Tunis, Sfax, Sousse, Monastir, and along main highways and coastal tourist areas. Very limited or no coverage in remote inland or desert regions.
  • Road Signage:
    • Motorways (Autoroutes): Green signs, marked with "A" + number (e.g., A1, A3). Connect major cities.
    • National Roads: Red signs with "RN" or just a number (e.g., RN1, RN8). Important highways but slower than autoroutes.
    • Regional Roads: Often marked with "C" + number (e.g., C92). These are more local roads, sometimes in poorer condition.
    • City Streets: Usually named in French (e.g., "Rue", "Avenue") or Arabic. Common formats: "Avenue Habib Bourguiba", "Rue de la Liberté".
Republic of Rwanda
  • Landscape: Known as the "Land of a Thousand Hills", Rwanda is very mountainous and hilly throughout. The west has steep mountains near Lake Kivu, central regions are rolling highlands, and the east is flatter with savannah and national parks. Vegetation is lush and green most of the year.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Google Street View launched in 2019. Coverage is mostly in Kigali and on key national roads and tourist routes (e.g., roads around Volcanoes National Park and Lake Kivu). Rural areas and village roads have little to no coverage.
  • Road Signage:
    • National Roads: Often marked as "NR" + number (e.g., NR1, NR4), though signage is not always consistent or frequent.
    • Other Roads: Smaller roads may be labeled "RR" (Regional Roads) or just local street names. Signage can be sparse outside cities.
    • City Streets: In Kigali, street signs are more common and follow formats like "KG 548 St" or "KN 3 Ave" – "KG" for Kigali Gasabo, "KN" for Kigali Nyarugenge, etc. Streets are usually numbered and follow a grid-like naming scheme in newer developments.
  • Google Street View Car: A white or gray pickup truck with a 360° camera system mounted on the bodywork. This vehicles was used for most of the Street View imagery in Rwanda
Kingdom of Eswatini
  • Landscape: A small but geographically diverse country. The west (Highveld) is mountainous and green, the central Middleveld is hilly and more populated, while the east (Lowveld) is flatter and drier with savannah-like terrain. Rivers and valleys are common, and the landscape can shift dramatically within short distances.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Google Street View coverage launched around 2020. Coverage is focused on main roads and urban areas like Mbabane (capital), Manzini, and major highways. Some tourist routes and national parks are also covered. Rural and mountainous regions often lack coverage.
  • Road Signage:
    • National Roads: Usually labeled with "MR" (Main Road) + number, e.g., MR3, MR7. These are the major highways connecting towns and border posts.
    • City Streets: In cities like Mbabane or Manzini, streets are sometimes named with English terms like "Main Street", "Church St.", or "Ngwane Park Rd." Signage is more visible in urban centers but still inconsistent.
Kingdom of Lesotho
  • Landscape: One of the most mountainous countries in Africa — the entire country lies above 1,400 m elevation. The east is dominated by the Maloti Mountains, with deep valleys and dramatic cliffs. The west is lower and more habitable but still hilly. Snow is common in winter at higher altitudes.
  • Street View Coverage: Official Google Street View launched around 2019–2020. Coverage is limited but includes major roads and cities like Maseru, along with some rural and mountainous roads that are accessible by car. Many smaller roads and remote villages are still not covered.
  • Road Signage:
    • Main Roads: Often marked as "A" + number (e.g., A1, A3). These connect major cities and border crossings.
    • Secondary Roads: May be labeled "B" + number (e.g., B25), though signage can be sparse in rural areas.
    • Local Roads: Usually unnamed or marked only on maps. Signage is rare in remote regions.
    • City Streets: In Maseru and other towns, street names are in English and often follow formats like "Kingsway", "Pioneer Rd.", "Old Europa Rd."
  • Google Street View Car: Typically a white Google Street View car
